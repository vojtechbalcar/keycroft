# Keycroft Design Context

## Design Context

### Users
- Adults and older learners who want to improve typing without feeling like they are using school software
- People who are bored by sterile, repetitive typing sites and want short sessions that feel satisfying instead of dutiful
- Returning users who should be able to drop in for a few minutes, make visible progress, and leave feeling momentum

### Brand Personality
- Cozy, premium, slightly magical
- Calm, grounded, and crafted rather than loud, corporate, or aggressively gamified
- The product should feel like a founder-made world with taste and restraint, not a generic edtech dashboard

### Aesthetic Direction
- Desktop-first, light-first, cream-forward visual system with warm neutrals, soft greens, and gentle natural accents
- A quiet village-restoration atmosphere: lived-in, warm, lightly magical, and emotionally adjacent to Stardew Valley without becoming rustic parody
- Marketing and hub surfaces can feel rich and atmospheric, while typing screens should stay cleaner, calmer, and lower-friction
- World growth should communicate real skill progress through refinement, detail, and liveliness instead of noisy reward spam
- The interface should explicitly avoid cold SaaS minimalism, childish classroom visuals, neon gamer styling, and pressure-heavy productivity aesthetics

### Design Principles
- Reward real improvement, not grind: progress visuals should reflect genuine typing gains rather than hollow streak mechanics or fake scarcity
- Preserve calm focus: every screen should keep one obvious next action and avoid clutter, especially during typing sessions
- Make progress feel tangible: users should quickly sense both immediate session impact and longer-term village development
- Stay warm but serious: the app can be inviting and magical without becoming cute, noisy, or juvenile
- Build for short sessions: entry, action, feedback, and return motivation should all work in a few minutes
